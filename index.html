Overview

The Election That Divided a Nation

The 2022 Philippine presidential election was one of the most polarising in recent memory — Ferdinand "Bongbong" Marcos Jr., son of the late dictator, ran against Leni Robredo, the incumbent vice-president. Marcos won with roughly 59% of the vote nationally, but the geographic distribution of that support was anything but uniform.

This page layers that electoral geography on top of regional economic data from the Philippine Statistics Authority (PSA). The central question: does wealth predict how a province voted?

The short answer is complicated. Some of the richest regions backed Marcos; some of the poorest did too. But Robredo's strongest provinces cluster in the Visayas — a pattern with deep roots in regional identity and historical memory.

Regional Breakdown

Average Marcos Vote Share by Region

Three patterns jump out:

  1. Ilocos Region (I) — Marcos's home turf — delivered the highest margins in the country, often exceeding 90%.
  2. BARMM voted heavily for Marcos despite being the poorest region, illustrating that voting behaviour in conflict-affected areas is shaped by factors well beyond income.
  3. Regions VI–VIII (the Visayas) are the only regions where Robredo averaged above 50% — consistent with her Visayas identity and the region's historical distance from the Marcos legacy.

Wealth vs. the Vote

Vote Distribution by Provincial Wealth Group

Across all four wealth groups, Marcos-won provinces outnumber Robredo-won ones — his dominance was not confined to any income bracket. The poorest quartile shows the widest spread, while the prosperous group (Metro Manila, CALABARZON, parts of Central Luzon) is more contested.

Notes on the Data

Methodology & Sources

Election figures are compiled from published COMELEC canvass results and cross-checked against independent media tallies. GDP per capita figures are PSA regional averages disaggregated proportionally to the province level using population weights — province-level national accounts are not officially published by PSA. All figures should be treated as indicative rather than official provincial statistics.

Province boundary GeoJSON sourced from macoymejia/geojsonph. Province name matching between the election dataset and the shapefile is resolved by normalised string comparison. The minor-candidate share (difference between Marcos% + Robredo% and 100%) is estimated as approximately 4–5 percentage points nationally.
